1. Gaussian distributions¶

1.1. Univariate Gaussian distribution¶

The probability density function for the univariate Gaussian distribution is

\begin{equation} \mathcal{N}\left(x|\mu, \sigma^2\right) = \frac{1}{\left(2\pi \sigma^2\right)^{1/2}} \exp\left(-\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}(x-\mu)^2 \right) \end{equation}

1.2. Multivariate Gaussian distribution¶

The probability density function for the \(n\)-dimensional multivariate Gaussian distribution is

\begin{equation} \mathcal{N}(x|{\mu}, \Sigma) = \frac{1}{\left(2\pi\right)^{N/2}|\Sigma|^{1/2}} \exp\left(- \frac{1}{2}(x - {\mu})^T \,\Sigma^{-1}({x} - {\mu}) \right) \end{equation}

for \(x\in \mathbf{R}^{n}\), the mean vector \(\mu\in \mathbf{R}^{n}\), and the covariance matrix \(\Sigma\in S_n^+\).

1.2.1. Properties of the covariance matrix \(\Sigma\)¶

1.2.1.1. Symmetry¶

It turns out that the covariance matrix \(\Sigma\) can be taken to be symmetric, without loss of generality. To show this, we first note that any quadratic form of the form \(x^T Ax\) is a scalar, hence symmetric with respect to \(A\),

\begin{equation*} x^TAx = \left(x^T A x\right)^T = x^T A^T x, \end{equation*}

since any scalar is equal to its own transpose. Second, recall the identity that any matrix can be decomposed into anti-symmetric and symmetric components

\begin{equation*} A = \frac{1}{2}\left(A - A^T\right) + \frac{1}{2}\left(A + A^T\right). \end{equation*}

Consider the antisymmetric part \(A - A^T\). Let \(z = (x-\mu)\).

\begin{equation*} z^T\left(A - A^T\right)z = z^T A z - z^T A^T z = 0 \end{equation*}

and the antisymmetric part of the matrix does not contribute to the exponential. Therefore the covariance \(\Sigma\) and the precision \(\Sigma^{-1}\) can be taken as symmetric without any loss of generality.

# for purposes of demonstration
class MVG(object):
    def __init__(self, μ, Σ):
        self.N = len(μ)
        self.μ, self.Σ = μ, Σ
        self.Σ_inv, self.Σ_det = np.linalg.inv(Σ), np.linalg.det(Σ)
        self.norml = 1/((2*π)**(self.N/2)*np.sqrt(self.Σ_det))

    def pdf(self, x):
        arg = -1/2*(x-self.μ).T @ self.Σ_inv @ (x - self.μ)
        pdf = self.norml * np.exp(arg)
        return pdf

1.2.2. Conditional and marginal distributions¶

Given a joint Gaussian distribution \(\mathcal{N}\left(x|\mu,\Sigma\right)\), and precision \(\Lambda \equiv \Sigma^{-1}\) and the partition

\begin{equation*} x = \begin{pmatrix} x_a \\ x_b \end{pmatrix},\qquad \mu = \begin{pmatrix} \mu_a \\ \mu_b \end{pmatrix}, \end{equation*}

\begin{equation*} \Sigma = \begin{pmatrix} \Sigma_{aa} & \Sigma_{ab} \\ \Sigma_{ba} & \Sigma_{bb} \end{pmatrix},\qquad \Lambda = \begin{pmatrix} \Lambda_{aa} & \Lambda_{ab} \\ \Lambda_{ba} & \Lambda_{bb} \end{pmatrix}, \end{equation*}

We provide a summary of the results; the derivations are algebra-heavy and appear in the appendix. The idea of all the proofs is to write out a partitioned vector and covariance matrix and expand the qaudratic form in the exponential, comparing like terms.

The conditional distribution \(p\left(x_a|x_b\right)\) is

\begin{align} p\left(x_a|x_b\right) &= \mathcal{N}\left(x| \mu_{a|b}, \Lambda_{aa}^{-1}\right) \\ \mu_{a|b} &= \mu_a - \Lambda_{aa}^{-1} \Lambda_{ab} \left(x_b - \mu_b\right) \end{align}

The marginal distribution \(p(x_a)\) is

\begin{equation} p(x_a) = \mathcal{N}\left(x_a | \mu_a, \Sigma_{aa}\right) \end{equation}

1.2.3. Bayes’ theorem for Gaussian variables¶

1.3. Appendix: Derivations¶

Maniuplation of Gaussian distributions is usually done up to a normalizing constant and involves algebraically grouping terms in the exponential.

1.3.1. Preliminaries¶

We will make repeated use of the generic form of the quadratic form in the exponential of a multivariate Gaussian distribution, to which we will often compare terms.

\begin{equation} \label{eq:gauss_QF} -\frac{1}{2} (x-\mu)^T \Sigma^{-1} (x-\mu) = -\frac{1}{2} x^T \Sigma^{-1} x + x^T \Sigma^{-1} \mu + \text{const}, \end{equation}

as well as the so called ‘Elliptic rectification identity’ which can identify a mean once we have the quadratic form in the exponential. This is sometimes referred to as “completing the square” for a Gaussian distribution.

\begin{equation} -\frac{1}{2} x^T A x + x^T v = -\frac{1}{2} \left(x - A^{-1}v\right)^T A \left(x - A^{-1}v\right) + \frac{1}{2} v^T A^{-1} v. \end{equation}

1.3.2. Partitioned Gaussian¶

The joint Gaussian distribution \(\mathcal{N}\left(x|\mu,\Sigma\right)\), and precision \(\Lambda \equiv \Sigma^{-1}\) can be expressed with the partition

\begin{equation*} x = \begin{pmatrix} x_a \\ x_b \end{pmatrix},\qquad \mu = \begin{pmatrix} \mu_a \\ \mu_b \end{pmatrix}, \end{equation*}

\begin{equation*} \Sigma = \begin{pmatrix} \Sigma_{aa} & \Sigma_{ab} \\ \Sigma_{ba} & \Sigma_{bb} \end{pmatrix},\qquad \Lambda = \begin{pmatrix} \Lambda_{aa} & \Lambda_{ab} \\ \Lambda_{ba} & \Lambda_{bb} \end{pmatrix}, \end{equation*}

\begin{align} -\frac{1}{2} \left(x -\mu\right)^T & \Lambda \left(x-\mu\right) \\ =&-\frac{1}{2} \left(x_a - \mu_a\right) \Lambda_{aa} (x_a - \mu_a) -\frac{1}{2} \left(x_a - \mu_a\right) \Lambda_{ab} (x_b - \mu_b) \\ &-\frac{1}{2} \left(x_b - \mu_b\right) \Lambda_{ba} (x_a - \mu_a) -\frac{1}{2} \left(x_b - \mu_b\right) \Lambda_{bb} (x_b - \mu_b) \label{eq:partitioned_quad} \end{align}

Which will be useful in algebraic manipulation of Gaussian distributions.

1.3.3. Conditional Gaussian distributions¶

A multivariate Gaussian conditioned on some of the components, \(p\left(x_a | x_b\right)\) is also a multivariate Gaussian distribution, because the joint distribution factors as \(p\left(x_a,x_b\right) = p\left(x_a|x_b\right)p\left(x_b\right)\). Therefore the conditional distribution \(p\left(x_a|x_b\right)\) is a re-normalization of the joint distribution. We are therefore justified in writing

\begin{equation*} p\left(x_a|x_b\right) = \mathcal{N}\left(x_a|\mu_{a|b}, \Sigma_{a|b}\right) \end{equation*}

where it remains to determine the parameters \(\mu_{a|b}\) and \(\Sigma_{a|b}\).

We use the quadratic form of the partitioned joint distribution in (\ref{eq:partitioned_quad}) and inspect the form of all terms involving \(x_a\). All other terms in the exponential are constant (since \(x_b\) is determined) and affect the normalization only.

We have to group all of the \(x_a\) terms, using the symmetry of the precision matrix \(\Lambda_{ab} = \Lambda_{ba}^T\) to write

\begin{align*} -\frac{1}{2} (x-\mu)^T\Lambda(x-\mu) &= -\frac{1}{2} x_a^T \Lambda_{aa} x_a + x_a^T \Lambda_{aa}\mu_a - x_a^T \Lambda_{ab} x_b + x_a^T \Lambda_{ab} \mu_b + \text{const}\\ &= -\frac{1}{2} x_a^T \Lambda_{aa} x_a + x_a^T\left(\Lambda_{aa}\mu_a + \Lambda_{ab}(x_b - \mu_b)\right) + \text{const} \\ &= -\frac{1}{2} x_a^T \Lambda_{aa} x_a + x_a^T \Lambda_{aa}\left(\mu_a + \Lambda_{aa}^{-1}\Lambda_{ab}(x_b - \mu_b)\right) + \text{const}. \\ \end{align*}

By comparing terms with the generic quadratic form associated with a generic multivariate Gaussian,

\begin{equation*} -\frac{1}{2} x^T \Sigma^{-1} x + x^T \Sigma^{-1} \mu + \text{const}, \end{equation*}

by inspection we have that the precision and the mean of the conditional distribution are

\begin{align} \Sigma_{a|b}^{-1} &= \Lambda_{aa} \\ \mu_{a|b} &= \mu_a + \Lambda_{aa}^{-1}\Lambda_{ab}(x_b - \mu_b). \end{align}

1.3.4. Marginal Gaussian distributions¶

The idea here is to evaluate the marginal distribution by direct integration

\begin{align} p(x_a) &= \int_{}^{} p\left(x_a,x_b\right) \,d x_b\\ &\propto \int_{}^{} \exp(\text{all terms}) \,d x_b\\ &= \exp\left(\text{non-} x_b \text{ terms}\right) \int_{}^{}\exp \left( x_b \text{ terms} \right) \,d x_b \end{align}
